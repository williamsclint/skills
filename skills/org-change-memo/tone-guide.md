# Tone Guide: Org Change Communication

## Core Principle
People read change memos with anxiety and skepticism. Your job is to reduce both — not by spin, but by being direct, honest, and human.

---

## Tone by Situation

### Reorg / Restructure (no job losses)
**Tone:** Clear and matter-of-fact, with warmth.
**Goal:** Reduce confusion about structure, reassure people their work is valued.
**Avoid:** Overloading with strategy speak. People care about their day-to-day.

### Layoffs / Role Eliminations
**Tone:** Somber, respectful, direct.
**Goal:** Treat affected people with dignity. Give remaining employees honest context.
**Avoid:** Euphemisms ("parting ways," "transitioning out"). Corporate language when people are losing jobs is perceived as cold and dishonest.
**Must include:** Acknowledgment that this is hard. What support is being offered to affected employees.

### Leadership Transition (departure or arrival)
**Tone:** Professional, forward-looking, respectful of the individual.
**Goal:** Stability. Signal continuity while acknowledging change.
**Avoid:** Overpromising about the incoming leader. Being vague about why someone left.

### Policy or Process Change
**Tone:** Informational, practical.
**Goal:** Clear understanding of what changes and what action is required.
**Avoid:** Over-explaining the rationale for minor changes. Under-explaining the rationale for significant ones.

### Performance-Driven Change (team restructure due to underperformance)
**Tone:** Measured, honest without being punitive.
**Goal:** Explain the change without scapegoating individuals.
**Avoid:** Naming individuals in a negative light. Being dishonest about the reason.

## Language Calibration

### Use instead of corporate speak:

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "Right-sizing" | "Reducing headcount" / "eliminating roles" |
| "Synergies" | "Working more efficiently together" |
| "Sunset" | "Discontinue" / "shut down" |
| "Transition" (as a euphemism for firing) | "Departure" / "let go" |
| "Strategic realignment" | "Changing our priorities" |
| "Impacted employees" | "Employees whose roles are being eliminated" |
| "Excited about the journey ahead" | (omit entirely if hollow) |
| "Learnings" | "Lessons" |

### Sentence structure
- Short sentences for hard news
- Longer sentences for context and rationale
- Bullet points for facts (what's changing, timeline, FAQ)
- Paragraphs for the human elements (why, what it means)

---

## Structural Anti-Patterns

**The buried lead:** Starting with 3 paragraphs of context before announcing the change. People scan for the news first — give it to them.

**The forced positive spin:** "We're excited to announce a restructuring." If it's hard news, don't frame it as exciting. People will resent the disconnect.

**The vague FAQ:** "Will my job be affected? We are evaluating all options." This non-answer generates more anxiety than no FAQ at all.

**The overcrowded memo:** Trying to address every scenario in one memo. Better to be clear about what you know now, and commit to a follow-up for what you don't.

**The passive voice coverup:** "Decisions were made..." Own the decision. "We decided..." or "I made the decision to..."

---

## Empathy Checkpoints

Before finalizing, ask:
- If I were a worried employee reading this, would I feel respected?
- Did I answer the questions people are actually asking?
- Is there anything in here I would be embarrassed to defend in a follow-up conversation?
- Have I given people a clear next step or point of contact?
- Does the closing feel genuine, or performative?
